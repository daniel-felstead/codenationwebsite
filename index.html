Introduction

About this page

Welcome to my homepage.

This website is a personal project of mine for a coding course.
I have tried to vary the content to cater for various interests. This homepage will blog and cover my time in Japan at the beginning of 2020.

It was a dream of mine to visit Japan for a long time, I have enjoyed anime since being a young teenager and developed a greater interest in the culture, values and etiquette as I grew older.

Fortunately my boyfriend (pictured with me right) had visited Japan twice before. He organised a holiday there with me which was an absolutely fantastic experience.

When setting out to travel there it was the second time I would head to the Asian continent and I needed to learn about the etiquette to not offend or disrespect the place I was visiting, a seemingly obvious endeavour but one that ended up with many complex cultural differences that needed remembering.

Etiquette basics

So what kind of etiquette do you need to remember?

If you buy food or drink from a vending machine don't walk around while eating/drinking - stop beside it, consume your purchase & place it into the provided bin.

If you are on a train or subway, mute and do not talk on your phone.

If you have tattoos (I do), you cannot usually visit an onsen - tattoos are still associated with the Yakuza.

If you are vegetarian you must carefully word how you state this.

Where about did I visit?

Tokyo

The first place in Japan we visited was Tokyo, a vast city with various quarters.

No matter where you went there was something for everyone. Despite the vast population and business I've never seen cleaner streets anywhere.

There multi-story shopping centres with floors upon floors of products (usually with a restaurant on top), hidden shrines in the middle of the metropolis and neon lights illuminating the way.

I felt safer here than any other major city I've visited in the world.

The abundance of shopping centres meant that you could be walking out of the subway and the stairs would lead you directly into a shopping centre.

Takayama

Takayama was the first "traditional" town we visited. It was a world-apart from Tokyo, with more antiquated architecture and a strong sense of historical preservation.

The streets were serene, the air extremely fresh and paths lead into the woods (warning signs of wild bears included) it really was the majestic and stereotypical Japan you'd expect from a samurai film.

This was also despite it being January, one of the only places we encountered snow. We also stayed in a ryokan, the traditional-style Japanese hotel, rather than a westernised one.

I was naive enough to opt for the traditional breakfast rather than the western continental.

Shirikawa-Go

This was the part of the trip I was most looking forward to not only was it a world heritage site, but it was also nested in snowy mountains.

It was wonderful to look around and also see a different cultures version of a thatched roof, despite being a world-apart from England similar historic techniques had been used to build houses.

I also, unlike in the ryokan had another traditional meal, this time absolutely delicious.

Kanazawa

Kanazawa was a return to a city, albeit a much smaller city than Tokyo and one I would argue felt more serene.

Of particular note was a water fountain that was a clock and as I would expect of Japan construction cones that were shaped like bears.

The shopping centres here were not as vast, but I did a lot of clothes shopping with much encouragement from my boyfriend.

Kyoto

The historic Kyoto was originally the capital of Japan, and unlike Tokyo was far more of a fusion of traditional and modern architecture.

I don't have a whole lot to say about Kyoto as we only spent a day here, much of it was spent trying to figure out how to meet our tour guide who was to take us to Nara.

Nara

Nara was hamazing! Upon our travel here we met with the "domesticated deer" who not only waited at zebra crossings with pedestrians, but also bowed politely back at you when you bowed.

There were rather amusing warning signs that they were still wild animals, but I'd never seen anything quite as amusing as their mannerisms.

We spent the day travelling through Nara park reaching a temple with gold Buddha statues.

Our guide Takako was extremely polite and well spoken, giving us time to stop and take pictures and even taking the picture featured at the top of this page.

Osaka

Osaka is affectionately refered to as the Manchester of Japan, I can truly see why; the people were warmer and less shy to conversation with foreigners, the districts had the same vibe and the attitude of shopkeepers was vastly more chatty.

It was a great city to end the trip on and had beautiful walks despite being busy.

Snapshots

Thinking to visit?

If you are thinking of visiting Japan: DO IT! I really can't recommend the experience enough, particularly if you've only previously visited western cultures. It's interesting culturally, historically and with a strong relevance in modern times, particularly in consumer electronics (although not to the futuristic cliche that the west imparts it). It really is a fantastic place to experience.

There's plenty about the trip that I haven't mentioned here, I would much encourage exploration and proper planning - see what you want to see, not the "main attractions".
